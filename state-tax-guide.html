🗺️ State Income Tax Guide for Freelancers (2026)

Your federal taxes are only half the picture. Most freelancers also owe state income tax. Here's what you need to know.

⚠️ Important: If you live in one state but work for clients in another, you may owe taxes to both. Consult a CPA for multi-state filing.

States with NO Income Tax (Freelancer-Friendly)

These 9 states do not tax wages or self-employment income:

  • 🇺🇸 Alaska
  • 🇺🇸 Florida
  • 🇺🇸 Nevada
  • 🇺🇸 New Hampshire* (taxes dividends & interest only)
  • 🇺🇸 South Dakota
  • 🇺🇸 Tennessee
  • 🇺🇸 Texas
  • 🇺🇸 Washington
  • 🇺🇸 Wyoming

*New Hampshire phases out interest/dividends tax by 2027.

States with Flat Income Tax (Easy to Calculate)

  • Colorado: 4.4% flat rate
  • Illinois: 4.95% flat rate
  • Indiana: 3.05% flat rate
  • Kentucky: 4.0% flat rate (decreasing)
  • Massachusetts: 5.0% flat rate (+ 4% surtax on income over $1M)
  • Michigan: 4.25% flat rate
  • North Carolina: 4.5% flat rate
  • Pennsylvania: 3.07% flat rate
  • Utah: 4.65% flat rate

Highest State Tax Rates (Top 5)

  • California: 1% – 13.3% (highest in US)
  • Hawaii: 1.4% – 11%
  • New York: 4% – 10.9%
  • New Jersey: 1.4% – 10.75%
  • Oregon: 4.75% – 9.9%

How to Pay State Quarterly Taxes

Most states require freelancers to make estimated quarterly payments, just like the IRS. Deadlines typically match federal dates (April 15, June 15, Sept 15, Jan 15).

Check your state's Department of Revenue website for:

  • State estimated tax vouchers (Form 1040-ES for your state)
  • Online payment portals
  • Penalty rates for late payment (typically 5-10% annually)

Quick Tips for Freelancers

  • 📌 If you moved mid-year, you may owe taxes to both states (partial-year resident filing).
  • 📌 Some cities have local income taxes (e.g., New York City, Philadelphia, Portland).
  • 📌 Freelancers in high-tax states should set aside an additional 5-10% for state payments.
